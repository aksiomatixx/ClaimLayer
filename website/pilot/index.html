Why it's different

Generic claims AI doesn't know California. This is built around the statute.

Most claims AI scores risk or summarizes documents and stops. ClaimLayer carries out the California-specific regulatory work — the math, the notices, the deadlines — inside guardrails enforced in code, with the model boxed in.

§4650 · §4061
The statutory clocks, computedFirst indemnity payment due within 14 days; §4650 adds an automatic 10% on late payments. ClaimLayer starts the right clock from receipt and tracks it on the correct calendar- vs business-day basis.
§5402
The 90-day compensability decisionCompensability runs on the §5402 clock — miss it and the claim is presumed accepted. The deadline is surfaced, not buried in a generic configurable diary.
§4610 · MTUS
Treatment authorization with no auto-denyEvery RFA is evaluated against MTUS. The AI path is constrained to approval or physician-review routing — denial is a physician-only action by law, and the model cannot reach it.
PDRS · AMA
Permanent-disability math the model can read but never inventPD reasoning is grounded in the 2005 PDRS and AMA Guides — apportionment and combined-values math from version-pinned DWC sources, not a number the model makes up.

Workflow

From raw document to prepared decision.

ClaimLayer is not a core-system replacement. It is the execution layer between inbound paperwork and the adjuster's decision surface.

01Classify incoming RFAs, work-status reports, medical reports, and legal documents against controlled categories.
02Match the document to its claim, or route low-confidence items to human triage — never silently filed.
03Draft the next action, compute the statutory deadline, and show the source document one click away.
04After human approval, document the decision, queue the notice, complete the diary, and set the successor deadline.
